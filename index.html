Introduction

On the surface, the advent of widespread Internet access and the improved communication capabilities that accompany it would seem to have heralded a new age of increasingly informed citizens.  With the wealth of human knowledge suddenly at the behest of your average citizen, one would think the fallacious claims could easily be countered with a matter of clicks.

However, this increased accessibility has opened the way for a new age of propaganda.  As more people are able to post professional-appearing content, the trust the general public has in news outlets has been exploited to further the goals of those presenting themselves as journalists while distancing themselves from the code of conduct professional journalists adhere to.

This presentation will take the issue of misinformation through various ethical frameworks, addressing how their principles relate to the moral implications of fake news.

Cultural Relativism

A cultural relativist holds that when viewing the products of an individual (be it beliefs, values, or practices), that individual’s culture should be the lens through which those products are evaluated.  This perspective should be applied to consuming news, as well.

While most cultures promote honesty, biased news is still present across all cultural groups. This is important to consider as a common tactic to avoid fake news is to get your news from foreign sources.  The idea behind this is that other countries will not have as much interest in political goals of your country’s domestic groups.   In many cases, this may hold true; however, in an increasingly connected world, one country’s citizens’ perception of another country may have implications on the original country’s political goals.  This particularly holds true if the country being written about has a prominent role on the world’s stage, such as the United States.

As such, it is important to consider where another culture’s (be it a different country or another culture within your own country) interest may lie when consuming news from that culture.

If anyone, no matter who, were given the opportunity of choosing from amongst all the nations in the world the set of beliefs which he thought best, he would inevitably—after careful considerations of their relative merits—choose that of his own country. Everyone without exception believes his own native customs, and the religion he was brought up in, to be the best.

- Herodotus, The Histories

Religion

Therefore, rid yourselves of all malice and all deceit, hypocrisy, envy, and slander of every kind.

- 1 Peter 2:1 (NIV)

We are not to lie, even if this might win us an advantage.

- Quran 16:92

Truth alone triumphs, not falsehood.

- Satyameva Jayate (Hindu Mundaka Upanishad)

As the verse listed here show, many religions hold deception as a dishonorable trait, even if it serves to benefit the religious group.  Misinformation, being generally spread with the intent of deceiving an audience for the benefit of whatever goal the creator is promoting, would be detested by religious groups.

That being said, studies suggest that religious people (along with members of any dogmatic group) are more likely to be susceptible to believing fake news (Bronstein, 2018).  This conflict between that tendency and what many religious texts advocate for would suggest that a good citizen who is also religious may need to be extra skeptical when receiving their news.

Note: The study cited here had a relative small sample size at just over 900 individuals, suggesting further research is needed to have a confident connection between religiosity and susceptibility to fake news.

Ethical Egoism

Ethical egoism is the one ethical framework that would perhaps be able to view misinformation as a morally permissible act.  An ethical egoist holds the position that one must do what is best for them, and in an age where anyone can post fake news in order to further their own interests, an ethical egoist would find that ability to be advantageous.

On the other hand, the self-interested nature of ethical egoism means a practitioner that is a consumer of fake news would find issue with another agent trying to further their ideals at the potential expense of their own.  As a result, an ethical egoist falling on this side of the dynamic may be more likely to practice skepticism to ensure they do not fall victim to someone else.  This is the silver lining that this framework provides in relation to misinformation.

Kantian Ethics

Immanuel Kant held an “absolute prohibition against lying” (Carson, 2010), a maxim resulting from Kant’s categorical imperative.  As such, someone operating from a Kantian perspective would view the dissemination of misinformation to always be wrong.

A Kantian consumer may be the one to speak out the loudest against misinformation on all sides due to their belief that there is no situation where that act is permissible.

In addition, most reputable new sources demand that their reporters and journalists strictly adhere to journalistic standards and ethics in order to strip the information they deliver from biases as much as possible.  Kant would approve.

Managing Misinformation in the Digital Age

Introduction

On the surface, the advent of widespread Internet access and the improved communication capabilities that accompany it would seem to have heralded a new age of increasingly informed citizens.  With the wealth of human knowledge suddenly at the behest of your average citizen, one would think the fallacious claims could easily be countered with a matter of clicks.

However, this increased accessibility has opened the way for a new age of propaganda.  As more people are able to post professional-appearing content, the trust the general public has in news outlets has been exploited to further the goals of those presenting themselves as journalists while distancing themselves from the code of conduct professional journalists adhere to.

This presentation will take the issue of misinformation through various ethical frameworks, addressing how their principles relate to the moral implications of fake news.

Cultural Relativism

A cultural relativist holds that when viewing the products of an individual (be it beliefs, values, or practices), that individual’s culture should be the lens through which those products are evaluated.  This perspective should be applied to consuming news, as well.

While most cultures promote honesty, biased news is still present across all cultural groups. This is important to consider as a common tactic to avoid fake news is to get your news from foreign sources.  The idea behind this is that other countries will not have as much interest in political goals of your country’s domestic groups.   In many cases, this may hold true; however, in an increasingly connected world, one country’s citizens’ perception of another country may have implications on the original country’s political goals.  This particularly holds true if the country being written about has a prominent role on the world’s stage, such as the United States.

As such, it is important to consider where another culture’s (be it a different country or another culture within your own country) interest may lie when consuming news from that culture.

If anyone, no matter who, were given the opportunity of choosing from amongst all the nations in the world the set of beliefs which he thought best, he would inevitably—after careful considerations of their relative merits—choose that of his own country. Everyone without exception believes his own native customs, and the religion he was brought up in, to be the best.

- Herodotus, The Histories

To effectively communicate, we must realize that we are all different in the way that we perceive the world and use this understanding as a guide to our communication with others.

- Anthony Robbins (Author and Philanthropist)

Religion

Therefore, rid yourselves of all malice and all deceit, hypocrisy, envy, and slander of every kind.

- 1 Peter 2:1 (NIV)

We are not to lie, even if this might win us an advantage.

- Quran 16:92

Truth alone triumphs, not falsehood.

- Satyameva Jayate (Hindu Mundaka Upanishad)

As the verse listed here show, many religions hold deception as a dishonorable trait, even if it serves to benefit the religious group.  Misinformation, being generally spread with the intent of deceiving an audience for the benefit of whatever goal the creator is promoting, would be detested by religious groups.

That being said, studies suggest that religious people (along with members of any dogmatic group) are more likely to be susceptible to believing fake news (Bronstein, 2018).  This conflict between that tendency and what many religious texts advocate for would suggest that a good citizen who is also religious may need to be extra skeptical when receiving their news.

Note: The study cited here had a relatively small sample size at just over 900 individuals, suggesting further research is needed to form a confident connection between religiosity and susceptibility to fake news.

Ethical Egoism

Ethical egoism is the one ethical framework that would perhaps be able to view misinformation as a morally permissible act.  An ethical egoist holds the position that one must do what is best for them, and in an age where anyone can post fake news in order to further their own interests, an ethical egoist would find that ability to be advantageous.

On the other hand, the self-interested nature of ethical egoism means a practitioner that is a consumer of fake news would find issue with another agent trying to further their ideals at the potential expense of their own.  As a result, an ethical egoist falling on this side of the dynamic may be more likely to practice skepticism to ensure they do not fall victim to someone else.  This is the silver lining that this framework provides in relation to misinformation.

Social Contract Theory

In societies that promote free speech and decry censorship, social contract theory is highly applicable.  To maintain social order, we hold each other accountable for each other’s actions.  This extends to journalistic integrity where we uphold the moral value of honesty.

We would not want to be deceived ourselves, and if we abide by social contract theory, we must also hold that we would not deceive others.  Often, the social contract is realized through legislation.  In recent years, we have seen many countries enact laws in order to curtail the spread of misinformation.  For example,

  • 2018 - Germany passes a law that holds social media platforms accountable for removing hate speech, including that which masquerades as news.
  • 2018 - France passes two laws giving judges the ability to order the removal of fake news during election campaigns.
  • 2019 - At the time of writing, Singapore is considering a law which requires Facebook to post warnings on content that government considers against the public interest.
(Reuters, 2019)

Utilitarianism

The focus Utilitarians have on increasing the overall happiness of people, along with the view that the interest of all should be considered equal, lends itself to promoting journalistic integrity.  Promoting the goals of a smaller group over the overall interests of the population is a definitively anti-utilitarian view.  Utilitarians would stand up against misinformation as a matter of moral principle.

It’s worth noting that some act-based variations of utilitarianism could be used to justify misinformation if the person believes that lying to achieve some goal would increase the overall happiness of people – the ends justify the means.  However, rule-based utilitarianism would find no way of justifying lying as honesty would undoubtedly be seen as the path that leads to the most happiness in the world.

Kantian Ethics

Immanuel Kant held an “absolute prohibition against lying” (Carson, 2010), a maxim resulting from Kant’s categorical imperative.  As such, someone operating from a Kantian perspective would view the dissemination of misinformation to always be wrong.

A Kantian consumer may be the one to speak out the loudest against misinformation on all sides due to their belief that there is no situation where that act is permissible.

In addition, most reputable new sources demand that their reporters and journalists strictly adhere to journalistic standards and ethics in order to strip the information they deliver from biases as much as possible.  Kant would approve.

Feminist Ethics

Feminist ethics concerns itself primarily with extending ethics to include women in the conversation, but in a larger sense it addressed the role power structures play in our formation of morality.  In many ways, this perspective is applicable to misinformation campaigns as they often seek to shift the power dynamics in favor of the group the author of the information is championing.

In addition, feminist ethics discusses how traditionally masculine traits, including autonomy, independence, and hierarchy dominate our moral discussions, where as traditionally feminine traits like connection, emotion, and trust are downplayed.  It is not uncommon, even today, for misinformation to be spread that disparages these traditionally feminine traits and those that stand for them.  Someone prescribing to feminist ethics would have a particular interest in fighting against misinformation as feminist, along with many marginalized groups that modern feminism also advocates for, are often the victim of fake news.

Note: this is of course not to say that a feminist is not capable of spread misinformation themselves.  We must always be aware that fake news can come from any group.

Conclusion

An important aspect of the human condition is the constant, unwavering pursuit of truth.  We are curious; we want to know.  A sound epistemology is an important utility in that pursuit and for navigating our complex world.  Information that comports with reality is useful information, because that is what allows us to make accurate decisions, specifically when faced with complex problems.

This is why we must be so cautious about misinformation.  Information is the most powerful tool we have in the modern age, and that has not gone unnoticed by those who would wish to utilize it for their own gain.  In order to mitigate this increasing issue, we must not only value critical thinking but also educate the public on how they can exercise that skill.  Rigorous research is a laborious task that many people will not naturally undertake for that reason.  However, the alternative is to allow others to make those decisions for us, and we cannot guarantee in that case that our best interests and the best interests of our society are being upheld.

My role in dealing with this issue is two-fold.  As a citizen, I feel I am obligated to question everything.   I cannot allow myself to fall into the bad habit of dishonestly dismissing dissenting opinions and unquestioningly upholding those that validate my own perspective.  This involves practicing the many subskills necessary for critical thinking, including checking multiple sources and looking for the signs of potential fake news, such as lack of references and clear authorship and looking into the research used to backup the article to check its validity (Kiely & Robertson, 2016).  As a developer, I may find myself in a position where the code that I write can impart biases on the content my products deliver.  Any algorithm I write or platforms I help create should be crafted in a way that prevents the spread of misinformation as best as possible.

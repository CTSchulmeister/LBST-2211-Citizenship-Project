Religion

Therefore, rid yourselves of all malice and all deceit, hypocrisy, envy, and slander of every kind.

- 1 Peter 2:1 (NIV)

We are not to lie, even if this might win us an advantage.

- Quran 16:92

Truth alone triumphs, not falsehood.

- Satyameva Jayate (Hindu Mundaka Upanishad)

As the verse listed here show, many religions hold deception as a dishonorable trait, even if it serves to benefit the religious group. Misinformation, being generally spread with the intent of deceiving an audience for the benefit of whatever goal the creator is promoting, would be detested by religious groups.

That being said, studies suggest that religious people (along with members of any dogmatic group) are more likely to be susceptible to believing fake news (Bronstein, 2018). This conflict between that tendency and what many religious texts advocate for would suggest that a good citizen who is also religious may need to be extra skeptical when receiving their news.

Note: The study cited here had a relative small sample size at just over 900 individuals, suggesting further research is needed to have a confident connection between religiosity and susceptibility to fake news.

Kantian Ethics

Immanuel Kant held an “absolute prohibition against lying” (Carson, 2010), a maxim resulting from Kant’s categorical imperative. As such, someone operating from a Kantian perspective would view the dissemination of misinformation to always be wrong.

Most reputable new sources demand that their reporters and journalists strictly adhere to journalistic standards and ethics in order to strip the information they deliver from biases as much as possible. Kant would approve.
